# AI & LLM Software Development Regulations — Mexico & Latin America

**Last Updated:** 2026-03-13
**Tags:** #ai-regulation #mexico #latin-america #brazil #chile #colombia #peru #software-development

---

## Overview

Latin America is in the early-to-middle stages of AI regulation. Most countries are pursuing risk-based frameworks influenced by the EU AI Act, but legislative progress varies widely. **Peru** is the only country in the region that has enacted an AI law. **Brazil** and **Chile** have the most advanced legislative proposals. **Mexico** has active proposals under Senate discussion. **Argentina** and **Colombia** have multiple bills in play. AI is projected to contribute about 5.4% of GDP (approximately USD 500 billion) to the region by 2030.

## Mexico

### Current Status
- **No enacted AI law** as of March 2026
- The **Federal Law Regulating Artificial Intelligence** (Ley Federal para la Regulación de la Inteligencia Artificial) is under discussion in the Senate Commission on Science and Technology
- Introduced in 2023 by Senator Alejandra Lagunes; final approval expected in 2026
- Multiple legislative proposals have been introduced and archived over recent years

### Proposed Framework
- **Risk-based classification** (aligned with EU AI Act and OECD principles)
- Creates the **National Commission for Artificial Intelligence (CONAIA)** as oversight body
- Mandates authorization, transparency, and accountability for high-risk AI systems
- Notably, Mexico's proposal **does not include an explicit prohibition** on unacceptable-risk AI (unlike most other LatAm proposals)
- Alignment with USMCA digital trade principles for cross-border operations

### Existing Legal Framework
- **Federal Law on Protection of Personal Data Held by Private Parties (LFPDPPP):** Data protection law that applies to AI systems processing personal data
- **Federal Consumer Protection Law:** Consumer rights apply to AI-powered services
- **Federal Copyright Law:** A 2025 Supreme Court (SCJN) ruling addressed AI-generated content, but the final published version excluded IP status of AI output — leaving the question unresolved
- Existing laws on discrimination, employment, and competition apply to AI use cases

### Copyright Uncertainty
- The SCJN's 2025 case raised questions about whether AI-generated content qualifies for copyright protection
- If AI output is deemed not copyrightable, it could be freely used/exploited without licenses — creating significant business uncertainty
- Final legal status remains unresolved

## Brazil

### Status: Most Advanced in the Region
- **PL 2338/2023** — Comprehensive AI bill passed by the Senate, now in the Chamber of Deputies
- Result of three years of legislative discussion with significant stakeholder participation
- Most detailed proposal in Latin America

### Key Features
- **Risk-based classification** with "excessive risk" (prohibited), high-risk, and general categories
- **Mandatory preliminary risk assessment** for all AI systems before market introduction
- Fundamental rights emphasis — transparency, explainability, accountability
- Specific sections on AI safety, algorithmic impact evaluations with detailed implementation methodology
- **ANPD** (National Data Protection Authority) as primary regulator
- **Regulatory Sandbox** — pilot program running until December 2026 for AI and data protection experimentation
- **Sector-specific regulation** active in judiciary (VICTOR, Sinapses systems), financial services, healthcare, and telecom

### Existing Laws
- **LGPD (General Data Protection Law):** Brazil's GDPR-equivalent applies to AI systems processing personal data
- **Consumer Defense Code:** Applies to AI in consumer-facing applications
- The ANPD actively monitors AI compliance and can impose fines

## Chile

### Status: Advanced Legislative Stage
- First National AI Policy published in 2021; updated policy released subsequently
- AI regulatory bill introduced in 2024 — focuses on promoting AI while respecting democratic principles and human rights
- Chile is considered a **regional leader** in the Latin American AI Index (CENIA, 2023)

### Key Features
- **Risk-based approach** with "unacceptable risk" prohibitions
- Requirements for AI developers, suppliers, deployers, and importers
- **Ministry of Science, Technology, Knowledge, and Innovation** oversees AI policy
- Emphasis on transparency, accountability, and human oversight
- National Center for Artificial Intelligence (CENIA) supports research and governance

### Strengths
- Strong data infrastructure and advanced human capital
- Active Ethical Algorithms Project
- 5G network rollout supporting AI adoption

## Peru

### Status: Only Enacted AI Law in the Region
- **Law No. 31814** — Approved by the President of the Council of Ministers in September 2025
- Promotes AI use for economic and social development
- Emphasizes human rights and ethical, sustainable, responsible AI use

### Three-Tier Risk Framework
| Tier | Description |
|------|------------|
| **Prohibited** | Manipulative practices, autonomous weapons, unauthorized mass surveillance, predictive policing |
| **High-Risk** | Enhanced oversight required |
| **Low-Risk** | Basic compliance requirements |

- Requires impact evaluations, transparency, and public registries
- Part of Peru's national digital transformation strategy

---

## Colombia

### Status: Active Legislative Process
- Senate approved **Bill 059 of 2023** in November 2023 — sets legal guidelines for AI development and use
- A newer comprehensive AI bill has also been introduced
- Multiple proposals have been introduced and archived over the years

### Key Features
- EU-inspired risk-based classification with "unacceptable risk" prohibitions
- Emphasis on regulatory sandboxes and compliance obligations
- Creates the **National Council for Artificial Intelligence (CON-IA)** — advisory body under the Office of the President
- Self-regulation, ethics, transparency, and technological sovereignty as guiding principles
- Requirements for data protection, intellectual property, and code of ethics
- Organizations must register with the Ministry of Science, Technology, and Innovation

## Argentina

### Status: Sector-Specific Approach
- **No comprehensive AI law enacted** — deliberate strategy to create a less regulated AI ecosystem to attract foreign investment
- **AI Strategy 2025** — non-binding governance guidelines
- Multiple AI bills under consideration in Congress
- Bills include "unacceptable risk" prohibitions, pre-market assessments, and bias mitigation

### Distinctive Approach
- Positioning as a potential AI innovation hub with minimal regulatory barriers
- Focus on AI applications in public administration and judiciary (e.g., Prometea predictive AI system)
- Financial services AI regulation developing through existing frameworks
- Data protection law includes right against automated decision-making by courts/administrative authorities

## Regional Trends and Patterns

### EU AI Act Influence ("Brussels Effect")
Most Latin American proposals have adopted risk-based classification systems directly influenced by the EU AI Act. This is particularly evident in Brazil, Chile, Colombia, Peru, and Argentina.

### Common Elements Across Jurisdictions
- Risk-based classification (prohibited → high-risk → low-risk)
- Transparency and explainability requirements
- Impact assessments for high-risk systems
- Human oversight obligations
- Data protection integration (all countries have comprehensive data protection laws)
- Rights against automated decision-making (Argentina, Peru, Mexico, Chile)

### Divergences
- **Mexico** lacks explicit prohibition on unacceptable-risk AI
- **Argentina** deliberately pursues lighter regulation for innovation
- **Brazil** has the most detailed implementation methodology
- **Peru** is the only country with enacted legislation

### Santiago Declaration
Adopted by Latin American and Caribbean nations, promoting ethical AI principles and regional cooperation on governance.

## What This Means for Developers

1. **Brazil is the highest priority** if you serve LatAm markets — its bill is the most advanced and comprehensive
2. **Mexico's law is expected in 2026** — monitor Senate progress and prepare for CONAIA oversight
3. **Peru's law is already in effect** — comply with its three-tier risk framework now
4. **Data protection laws apply everywhere** — LGPD (Brazil), LFPDPPP (Mexico), etc. cover AI processing of personal data
5. **Risk classification** will likely be required across the region — start categorizing your AI systems now
6. **Copyright is unsettled** across the region — AI training data and output ownership lack clear rules
7. **Regional consistency is increasing** — EU AI Act alignment means compliance with EU requirements will largely transfer
8. **Argentina's lighter approach** may attract AI startups, but existing data protection still applies
9. **Cross-border operations** benefit from USMCA alignment (Mexico) and regional data transfer frameworks
