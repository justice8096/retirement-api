# AI & LLM Software Development Regulations — Australia

**Last Updated:** 2026-03-13
**Tags:** #ai-regulation #australia #privacy-act #software-development

---

## Overview

Australia does **not** have standalone AI-specific legislation. Instead, the government relies on existing technology-neutral laws (privacy, consumer protection, anti-discrimination) supplemented by voluntary guidance frameworks. The December 2025 National AI Plan confirmed this approach — pausing work on mandatory guardrails in favor of leveraging existing regulatory infrastructure and establishing an AI Safety Institute.

---

## Regulatory Approach

Australia's posture has evolved through several phases:

| Year | Development |
|------|------------|
| 2019 | AI Ethics Principles published (8 voluntary principles) |
| 2024 | Voluntary AI Safety Standard (VAISS) with 10 guardrails released; Mandatory guardrails for high-risk AI proposed |
| Oct 2025 | Guidance for AI Adoption (AI6) replaces VAISS as primary government guidance |
| Dec 2025 | **National AI Plan** released — confirms reliance on existing laws, no standalone AI legislation |
| Early 2026 | **AI Safety Institute** becomes operational |

## Key Governance Frameworks (Voluntary)

### Guidance for AI Adoption (AI6) — October 2025
Replaces the VAISS. Sets out six essential practices for responsible AI governance:
- Accountability and governance structures
- Risk identification and management
- Data quality and integrity
- Testing, monitoring, and evaluation
- Transparency and explainability
- Human oversight and control

Two versions available: **Foundations** (for organizations starting out) and **Implementation Practices** (for more mature organizations). Supported by an AI screening tool, policy guide/template, and AI register template.

### AI Ethics Principles (2019)
Eight voluntary principles aligned with OECD AI Principles:
1. Human, societal, and environmental wellbeing
2. Human-centered values
3. Fairness
4. Privacy protection and security
5. Reliability and safety
6. Transparency and explainability
7. Contestability
8. Accountability

## Existing Laws That Apply to AI

### Privacy Act 1988 (Cth) — Key AI Implications
- The primary data protection framework; currently undergoing significant reform
- **2024 amendments** introduced transparency obligations around automated decision-making — **effective December 2026**
- Organizations using AI with personal information must comply with Australian Privacy Principles (APPs)
- Maximum penalties: up to **AUD 3.3 million** for privacy interference; AUD 333,000 for infringement notice breaches
- The OAIC has published guidance on privacy considerations for AI product selection and generative AI model development

### Australian Consumer Law (Competition and Consumer Act 2010)
- The ACCC actively monitors AI's impact on competition and consumer protection
- Misleading or deceptive conduct rules apply to AI-generated content and recommendations
- AI-driven pricing algorithms face scrutiny

### Anti-Discrimination / Human Rights
- The Australian Human Rights Commission addresses AI fairness
- Employment discrimination laws apply to AI-driven recruitment and HR decisions
- Algorithmic bias can trigger complaints under federal and state discrimination laws

### Online Safety Act
- The eSafety Commissioner has acted against "nudify" AI apps
- AI chatbots interacting with children are subject to eSafety industry codes
- Non-compliance can result in financial penalties and enforcement action

### Sector-Specific Regulation
- **Healthcare:** TGA regulates AI medical devices under therapeutic goods framework
- **Financial Services:** APRA and ASIC oversight applies to AI in banking/insurance
- **Employment:** Fair Work Commission jurisdiction covers algorithmic management
- **Telecommunications:** ACMA regulatory expectations extend to AI in broadcasting/telecoms

## AI Safety Institute (AISI)

Announced November 2025, funded with AUD 29.9 million, operational from early 2026:
- Independent technical analysis of AI capabilities and risks
- Safety testing and monitoring of advanced AI systems
- Advice to regulators and ministers on emerging risks
- Coordination with international AI safety commitments (Five Eyes, OECD)
- Guidance on AI opportunity, risk, and safety through existing channels

## Copyright and AI

The copyright landscape remains unsettled:
- **No text and data mining (TDM) exemption** — the Attorney-General ruled this out in October 2025
- Using copyrighted works to train AI models remains legally ambiguous
- AI cannot be an "inventor" for patent purposes (Thaler v Commissioner of Patents, 2022)
- No clear rule on whether AI-generated outputs are copyrightable
- Ongoing consultations on licensing and fair remuneration for creators
- Productivity Commission recommended monitoring for 3 years before legislative review

## State-Level Developments

### New South Wales
- **Work Health and Safety Amendment (Digital Work Systems) Bill 2025** introduced to parliament
- Targets algorithms, AI, automation, and online platforms
- Would establish a "primary duty of care" for businesses to ensure workers' health and safety isn't compromised by digital systems

---

## Public Trust Challenge

- Only **30%** of Australians believe AI benefits outweigh risks (2025 University of Melbourne/KPMG study)
- **78%** expressed concern about negative AI outcomes
- Only **30%** believe current laws and safeguards are adequate
- Only **36%** trust AI systems broadly

---

## What This Means for Developers

1. **No AI-specific compliance regime yet** — but existing laws apply broadly
2. **Privacy Act reforms (Dec 2026)** will impose automated decision-making transparency obligations — prepare now
3. **Align with AI6 practices** voluntarily to demonstrate good governance and readiness for future mandatory rules
4. **Consumer law applies** — AI-generated content must not be misleading or deceptive
5. **Sector-specific rules matter** — health, financial, and employment AI face additional scrutiny
6. **Watch for the AI Safety Institute** outputs in 2026 for emerging guidance
7. **Copyright caution** — training on Australian copyrighted material without licenses carries legal risk
